Nowadays, our security and privacy are strongly threatened by malware programs which aim to steal our 
conﬁdential data and make our systems out of service, among other things. While traditional signature-based 
malware detection methods or statistical analysis have proven to be ineﬀective and time-consuming, recently 
data-driven Artiﬁcial Intelligence(AI) techniques, i.e. Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) approaches, 
have been successfully applied leveraging the behavior of malware in terms of API calls, and achieving 
promising performances. However, their black-box behavior leads to a lack of explainability thus preventing 
their application in real world scenarios. In light of this, eXplainable Artiﬁcial Intelligence (XAI) methodologies 
and tools can be eﬀectively embedded within an AI-based malware detection process in order to make more 
understandable the produced results. In this paper, we propose a XAI framework for behavioral malware 
detection problems and evaluate the usefulness of four XAI methods (SHAP, LIME, LRP and Attention mechanism) 
on three datasets with diﬀerent size, sequence length and number of classes, by which we could evaluate the 
strengths and weaknesses – from eﬀectiveness and eﬃciency point of views – of recurrent deep architectures 
(i.e. Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) models), and their applicability in the 
modern Cyber Security(CS) scenarios.
1. Introduction
In the last decade, the number and variety of cyber attacks to In-
formation Systems of organizations or single individuals has steadily 
grown, mainly due to the diﬀusion of Internet of Things (IoT) devices 
(Fei et al. (2023)) and Cloud Computing (Karn et al. (2021)).
Cyber criminal attacks may have countless purposes, including the 
insertion of advertising and purchase oﬀers in websites, the denial of 
service of computing systems or the stealing of conﬁdential data for ﬁ-
nancial gains. Generally, the attackers leverage malicious software (e.g. 
trojan, backdoor, spyware, worm), also known as malware, to cause se-
rious threats for the vulnerability of systems (Yan et al. (2023)).
The detection of malware is an extremely challenging research task 
because as Cyber Security (CS) researchers develop new techniques, 
malware authors improve their ability to evade detection (Galloro et al. 
(2022); Zhong et al. (2023)). From this perspective, the Cyber Threat 
Intelligence (CTI) process aims to analyze and monitor the huge vol-
umes of cyber security information (e.g. execution logs, traﬃc logs) to 
enhance the security of organizations’ systems (Sun et al. (2023)).

Traditional antivirus software programs cannot constitute an eﬀec-
tive and timely solution to malware detection, since they are designed 
to detect viruses by searching their signature within a database which 
is updated from time to time (signature-based malware detection(Bazraf-
shan et al. (2013))). Such approaches are easily escaped with advanced 
evasion techniques, such as code obfuscation(Cheng et al. (2021)).
From a more structured perspective, malware signatures can be 
analyzed in a static or dynamic fashion: while Static Malware Detec-
tion (SMD) approaches focus on the software’s byte-code, Behavioral 
Malware Detection (BMD) analyses the behavior of the software at run-
time, i.e. the sequence of Application Programming Interface (API) calls 
which the software makes to the operating system (Alazab et al. (2010)) 
(refer to Fig.1for an example). Speciﬁcally, API is a set of protocols and 
functions to exchange data between diﬀerent devices and applications 
(as shown in the example of Fig. 1), while a system call provides an 
interface between the underlying operating system and the program. 
Hereafter, we refer to API-calls and system calls as synonyms. The com-
plex and subtle behavior of modern malwares makes SMD vulnerable 
to several attacks (e.g. dynamic code linking and encryption)(Gaber  et 
al. (2023)) and explains the increasing interest in designing (semi-)au-

Gaber et al. (2023)).
Despite their outstanding performance, these approaches usually 
result in complex models that neither their designers are able to under-
stand. While maintaining high levels of learning performance, it would 
be good —  and sometimes mandatory —  to guarantee the explainability 
of models, i.e. their ability to be understood and appropriately trusted 
by humans (Ali et al. (2023)).
Consequently, we are assisting to a more and more pervasive diﬀu-
sion of eXplainable Artiﬁcial Intelligence(XAI) methodologies and tools, 
even in the cyber-security domain (Zhang et al. (2022)). When con-
sidering malware analysis, the explainability requirements have been 
surveyed in the context of Android malware defense models (Liu et al. 
(2022a); Fan et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022b)) and 
some explainable approaches have been proposed for SMD (Fan et al. 
(2021); Liu et al. (2022b)).
However, the explainabilityproblem  for BMD systems is still under-
explored, especially when the detection model employs modern deep 
sequential neural networks. In light of this, our analyses consider the se-
quential behavior of a malware, which is diﬀerent from the traditional 
static approach based on feature extraction as demonstrated in (Fan et 
al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022b)). Additionally, while pre-
vious work (Fan et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022b)) only 
focused on classifying Android applications as malware or not, our ap-
proach provides a more comprehensive view by investigating a broader 
range of systems. Concretely, XAI could support a variety of analyses 
of malware’s behavior, e.g. by ranking its API calls according to how 
important they were for the model to make its prediction. Eventually, 
the explanation might help the analyst to understand the pivotal mal-
ware operations along with the aﬀected resources. A simple example is 
shown in Fig.1: the explanation suggests that the most relevant API con-
sidered by the model to classify the software behavior as a ransomware
is InternetOpenUrlA.
In this paper, we present a XAI framework for BMD, aiming to eval-
uate the eﬀectiveness, strengths, and weaknesses of four distinct XAI 
methods. We compare explanations provided by Local Interpretable 
Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME), SHapley Additive exPlanation 
(SHAP), Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP), and attention mech-
anisms when explaining predictions from LSTM and GRU models. This 
model processes API call sequences as input and outputs malware 
class predictions. We quantitatively assess the similarity of explana-
tions provided by diﬀerent techniques and validate the eﬀectiveness 
of explanations by evaluating how predictions change when inﬂuen-
tial features are retained or non-inﬂuential features are removed. Our 
experiments span three publicly-available datasets with varying sizes, 
sequence lengths, and class numbers, revealing the eﬃcacy of each 
method under diverse experimental perspectives despite disagreements 
between explanations.
The paper is organized as follows: Section2discusses related work 
on deep learning techniques applied to malware detection and some 
preliminary works considering XAI in this domain. Section3contains a 
comprehensive description of LSTMs and the explanation techniques 
evaluated throughout this work, while a detailed description of our 
framework is provided in section 4. Our experiments and results are 
detailed in Section 5. Finally, Section6 reports conclusions and possi-
ble future work.
2. Related work
The data-driven nature of the CTI process has determined an increas-
ing interest in applying machine learning and deep learning approaches 
to support its activities(Mills  et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023)). In partic-
ular, the detection of malware activities is becoming a challenging task 
due to the explosive growth in the number of connected devices and the 
evolution of attack strategies(Fei  et al. (2023)).
In the last years, an increased number of works is focusing on BMD 
due to the robustness of such approaches to several types of attacks (e.g. 
code obfuscation, dynamic code linking and encryption) (Gaber et al. 
(2023)). Common deep learning methods dealing with the dynamic na-
ture of system calls are based on Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), 
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), LSTMs, and Graph Convolutional 
Networks (GCNs)(Gopinath  and Sethuraman (2023)). Table1summa-
rizes the state-of-the-art BMD approaches categorized by classiﬁcation 
model.
Despite having proven promising detection performance, the afore-
mentioned methods could not be used in real scenarios due to the lack 
of explainability caused by their complexity; as a matter of fact, the 
complexity of AI models and their behaviors is often obscure to end 
users having to make decisions based on AI-based predictions. Conse-
quently, researches have now converged into the XAI ﬁeld, whose aim 
is to produce intelligent agents able to provide explanations for their 
decisions(Saeed  and Omlin (2023); Ali et al. (2023)). In particular, the 
term explainability refers to the ability to explain or to visualize an AI 
model’s decision in understandable terms to humans (e.g. Rong et al. 
(2023)).
Various works investigate the explainability issue in malware detec-
tion: (Melis et al. (2018)) propose a gradient-based approach to identify 
the most inﬂuential features in the detection process;(Han  et al. (2019)) 
investigate the correlation between static and dynamic features that 
aﬀect prediction performance about diﬀerent malware families. Fur-
thermore, (Warnecke et al. (2020)) evaluate six explanation methods 
aiming to unveil the main features in deep learning models for malware 
detection.
However, to the best of our knowledge, the explainability problem 
has not been investigated yet in the BMD domain. In fact, our analyses 
on explanation methods consider the sequential behavior of a malware, 
which is diﬀerent from the classical static approach based on feature 
extraction as demonstrated in (Fan et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); 
Liu et al. (2022b)). Additionally, while current literature (Fan et al. 
(2021); Wu et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022b)) mainly focuses on Android 
malware, our approach provides a more comprehensive view. In this 
paper, we explore the application of a comprehensive set of explana-
tion techniques on top of LSTM-based deep learning models because of 
their wide use and high performance on several datasets and contexts 
(Jahromi et al. (2020)), also conﬁrmed by our experimental analysis. 
In particular, we compared the explanations provided by four diﬀer-
ent state-of-the-art XAI methods, relying on feature importance, with 
three diﬀerent datasets varying in size, sequence length and number of 
classes.

3. Theoretical background
In this section we introduce some basic notions, which our work 
relies on. In particular, we ﬁrstly describe LSTMs —  the deep learning 
technique at the core of our malware classiﬁers —  and we successively 
focus on the explanation techniques analyzed throughout this paper.
3.1. Recurrent neural networks
As mentioned in the previous sections, behavioral malware detec-
tion deals with API calls resulting from the execution of malware and 
goodware instances in sand-boxed environments. The sequential nature 
of these data makes RNNs and LSTMs particularly suitable to perform 
the detection task. The general idea behind these models is to introduce 
a dependence mechanism between consecutive time-steps, e.g. a mali-
cious behavior is never determined by an API call in isolation, but by 
the sequence in which it takes place.
Speciﬁcally, LSTMs (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997)) are an 
evolution of RNNs designed to learn long-term dependencies. Speciﬁ-
cally, each time step 𝑡, i.e. each API call in the sequence, is processed 
through a memory cell 𝐶𝑡which store the information to remember in 
the successive time-steps. 𝐶𝑡’s values are controlled with three gates: 
forget 𝑓𝑡, input 𝑖𝑡and output 𝑜𝑡gates. In turn, 𝑓𝑡and 𝑖𝑡control 𝐶𝑡to 
decide what information should actually be thrown away and added, 
respectively. Finally, 𝑜𝑡ﬁlters which part of 𝐶𝑡should be passed to 
the next time-step. For instance, consider the API (sub-)sequence Nt-
TerminateProcess → NtOpenFile → NtWriteFile and assume the current 
time-step is the second API call (𝑡 =2). Under this settings, 𝑓2 could de-
cide to forget the previous one if it terminates a not-related process, 𝑖2
might choose to store the target ﬁle information and 𝑜2 could decide 
to output only the ﬁle privileges which is important to understand the 
legitimacy of the successive writing operation.
Based on the above-mentioned concepts, some LSTM variants, such 
as Bidirectional-LSTM (BiLSTM), Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), have 
been proposed (Li et al. (2022b)). In particular, BiLSTM is a sequence 
processing model that consists of two LSTMs one taking the input in a 
forward direction and the other in a backwards direction, while GRU 
has only two gates that are reset and update making it less complex 
than LSTM in terms of parameters.
3.2. Explanation methods
Since our work is the ﬁrst one focusing on XAI for behavioral mal-
ware detection, we decide to investigate strengths and weaknesses of 
the most famous state-of-the-art XAI techniques. Since there is no agree-
ment on the best techniques, nor a standard for the explanation genera-
tion, we explore the whole XAI spectrum to assess whether in behavioral 
malware detection domain some techniques could be preferred to the 
others.
Concretely, we analyze explanations provided by LIME (Ribeiro et 
al. (2016)), SHAP (Lundberg and Lee (2017)), LRP (Bach et al. (2015)) 
and attention mechanism (Bahdanau et al. (2015)) on LSTM-based clas-
siﬁcation models. The ﬁrst two methods are post-hoc, i.e. they are 
applied after the model is built and trained, and model-agnostic, i.e. 
they look at 𝑓as it were a black-box, considering only its inputs and 
outputs. Practically, they can be useful when explaining a third-party 
model (such as those provided on Microsoft and Amazon clouds), only 
accessible on-demand. On the other hand, LRP, despite being a post-hoc 
technique, is a white-box technique since it requires the access to 𝑓’s in-
ner structure. Concretely, it is suitable in debugging scenarios where the 
domain expert could assess what the model is learning. Finally, the at-
tention mechanism requires changing 𝑓’s structure and thus re-training 
the whole system. An overview of the techniques is shown in Fig.2.
The XAI methods studied in this work represent a diverse range of 
techniques. LIME focuses on local model explainability through perturb-

consecutive sub-sequences which determine the malware capability to 
harm the system. In the remainder of this section, we provide some 
details of the above-mentioned explanation methods.
3.2.1. Local interpretable model-agnostic explanations

interpretable surrogate model which approximates the complex behav-

how the decision function is complex, it can be approximated by a lin-
ear model in the neighborhood of the instance being the explanation 
target.

model 𝑓, LIME ﬁnds an explainable model 𝑔(e.g. linear model) by 
minimizing the following objective function:

domains including text and/or sequence classiﬁcation (Ribeiro et al.

even considering LSTM models. Interestingly, (Liu et al. (2022b)) shows 
the promising performance of LIME explanations in the context of SMD.
3.2.2. Shapley additive exPlanations
SHAP (Lundberg and Lee (2017)) approximates the computation of 
Shapley values, a statistical method from coalition game theory (Shap-
ley et al. (1988)), applied in diﬀerent application domains (i.e., text 
classiﬁcation (Kokalj et al. (2021)) and cryptomining detection (Karn 
et al. (2021))). At high level, features, i.e. the API calls in a sequence, 
are considered as “players” which cooperate in coalitions to obtain a 
proﬁt and the prediction of the black-box model 𝑓provides the “pay-
out” which has to be distributed among players. Shapley values are the 
marginal contribution of feature values across all possible coalitions. In 
other words, the i-th Shapley value represents the contribution of the 
i-th API call in the sequence for the prediction.
Speciﬁcally, three methods are proposed: (i) kernelSHAP is the 
model-agnostic version of the algorithm working only with inputs and 
outputs of 𝑓, (ii) treeSHAPcomputes the Shapley values assuming 𝑓is 
a tree-based classiﬁcation model, (iii) deepSHAP optimizes the compu-
tation for neural network architectures.

3.2.4. Attention layer
Initially designed in the context of Machine TranslationBahdanau  et 
al. (2015), the attention mechanismhas been applied in many diﬀerent 
domains, from document classiﬁcationYang  et al. (2016)to  scene seg-
mentationChen  et al. (2023)to  medical diagnosisBarata  et al. (2021). 
In the context of text classiﬁcation, its aim is to select the most relevant 
parts of context information to facilitate learning, assigning a score to 
each token of the sequence. Formally, we adopt the soft attention mech-
anism proposed inYang  et al. (2016).
It is worth to note that more powerful attention mechanisms, i.e. 
self-attention and multi-head attention Vaswani et al. (2017), have been 
recently proposed in NLP literature to learn meaningful representations 
even in unsupervised settings. We will compare these approaches in the 
experimental section.
However, according to the XAI taxonomy, the attention mechanism 
is not a post-hoc method, i.e. it requires the model to be built with an 
attention layer and hence trained on available training data.
Concretely, the attention scores reﬂect the relevance of each token 
for the prediction, thus being comparable with scores provided by the 
others feature importance-based techniques. However, it is worth to 
note that the contextual representation of the LSTM does not guaran-
tee similar context vectors for similar sequences Du et al. (2019), thus 
representing a bias with respect to the other XAI post-hoc methods.

4.2. Our framework
Our methodology builds upon a pipeline composed by three steps: 
the sequence pre-processingmodule aims to standardize the data format, 
the model is a classiﬁcation learner which exploits the sequence struc-
ture of input data to perform the classiﬁcation and the explainer gen-
erates the explanation supporting the model’s prediction. Our method-
ological workﬂow is summarized in Fig.3.
4.2.1. Sequence pre-processing
The original sequences collected from the sandbox execution suﬀer 
from diﬀerent problems concerning indeﬁnite sequence length, over-
lapping subsequences and blurred boundary of sequence from malware 
and benign software. We propose to perform the following operations:
•D e c r e a s e redundancy in the sequences by keeping only the ﬁrst 
API call in a sub-sequence of identical calls in order to capture 
malware behavioral diﬀerences more clearly in sequence order. 
For instance, a malware attack could hide its core malicious sub-

4.2.2. Model architecture
We consider classiﬁcation models which should be able to process 
sequence data so as to extract knowledge from the API calls performed 
by malware/goodware. Speciﬁcally, we propose to use an LSTM- and 
GRU-based architectures, as depicted in Fig. 3. Given the ﬁxed-length

4.2.3. Explainer
The explainer module works with the model and the instance to 
explain (i.e. target instance) in order to extract the explanation 𝐸as 
deﬁned in the previous section. Depending on the explainer typology, 
there would be some considerations to be made: (1) model-speciﬁc 
techniques (Attention mechanism and LRP) force to use the classiﬁ-
cation model they are designed for, (2) white-box approaches (LRP) 
require the access to the internal states of the model, (3) model-agnostic 
methods (LIME, SHAP) do not rely on the classiﬁcation model but may 
still require the dataset used for the training procedure. Consequently, 
diﬀerent techniques may lead to dissimilar (even inconsistent) explana-
tions since they make diﬀerent assumptions and use diverse artifacts of 
the training stage.
5. Experimental evaluation
This section discusses the obtained results according to the experi-
mental protocol, aiming to evaluate the proposed framework in terms 
of running time and eﬃcacy with both quantitative and qualitative 
measures. In addition, we released our code in a github repository1
to guarantee its replicability.
5.1. Experimental protocol
We evaluated both the eﬃciency and the eﬀectiveness of the expla-
nation methods described in Section3.
Since the growing interest in applying of XAI methodologies for dif-
ferent application domains (e.g., propaganda detection (Cavaliere et al. 
(2023))), we focused our analysis from diﬀerent points of view.
In particular, on the one hand, we compared the run timerequired to 
provide explanations (Section5.3), on the other hand, the eﬀectiveness 
of the diﬀerent techniques has been assessed on both qualitative and

explainers are similar between each other by means of a compati-

•t h e Sentence-Level Representation approach proposed by (Arras et 
al. (2019)) has been applied to investigate whether it is possible to 
retrieve structured API call sequence representations through the 
scores provided by the analyzed techniques (Section5.4.2);

5.2. Classiﬁcation results
Datasets have been split in training (60%), validation (20%) and test 
sets (20%) in a stratiﬁed manner in order to perform the training and 
performance evaluation. For our evaluations, we used the most com-
mon metrics for classiﬁcation tasks Pendleton et al. (2016): i) Accuracy, 
which is the correct predictions ratio; ii) Precision, which measures the 
proportion of truly malicious API calls among all the detected samples; 
iii) Recall, which quantiﬁes the number of detected malicious API calls 
among all of them, quantifying the false negative rate; iv) 𝐹1 score, 
which measures the overall eﬀectiveness by combining precision and 
recall.
Table 4 reports the performance for each dataset and model we 
tested (refer to the Appendix for detailed metrics for each class). The 
(MultiHead) attention performance refers to a diﬀerent model, trained 
with the (multi-head) attention layer after the LSTM.
Speciﬁcally, the LSTM achieves better results, in terms of 𝐹1score, 
than GRU and BiLSTM on two out of three datasets. Consequently, we 
chose this model as the one to be explained by post-hoc XAI techniques, 
i.e. LIME, SHAP and LRP.

In addition, multi-head attention seems not beneﬁcent since it always 
leads to worse metrics. This situation can be also found in (Wiegreﬀe 
and Pinter (2019)), where the authors deemed the datasets unsuitable

5.3. Eﬃciency analysis
When performing malware detection, analysts should understand 
what is happening in the system as soon as possible, before the mal-
ware makes more damages or spreads across the network. From this 
perspective, the explanation could speed up the process if its genera-
tion is time eﬃcient; in other words, even if the explanation could help 
the analysts in understanding what has happened, it is useless if its gen-
eration requires too much time.
To this end, we evaluate the running times of the analyzed explain-
ers for each dataset. It is worth to note that for post-hoc techniques 
(LRP, LIME and SHAP) the explanation time must be added to the pre-
diction one.
Table 5 reports the mean run times and the 95% conﬁdence inter-
vals computed considering the instances of the testing sets for each 
dataset (1, 500 samples for Alibaba, 1, 422 samples for Mal-API-2019 
and 8, 776 samples for API Call Sequences, respectively). Not surpris-
ingly, LRP achieves by far the best results across all datasets, 0.12, 0.17 
and 0.99 seconds respectively. The reason behind this performance is 
explained by the fact that it just requires a single back-propagation step 
along the network model, in contrast to other explainers which require 
ﬁtting either a statistical model (SHAP) or a transparent machine learn-
ing algorithm (LIME). In any case, the running times on the Alibaba 
dataset (0.99 seconds) are quite worse because of the longer input API 
sequences.
The Attention running times are not reported since attention mech-
anism is included into the network, so the explanations are computed 
simultaneously with the prediction. Interestingly, SHAP performance 
strongly depends on the instances on which the explainer is built. To 
perform the experiment, we set the maximum number of training sam-
ples that our machine could handle.

5.4.3. Perturbation experiment
We perform a perturbation experiment, a.k.a. “pixel-ﬂipping” in com-
puter vision(Samek  et al. (2016)), by perturbing system calls from the 
input sequences according to their relevance values, and tracking the 
impact on the classiﬁcation performance (Qiu et al. (2022)). This quan-
titative analysis aims to answer two questions:
•w e r e the features extracted from the explanation the ones the 
model looked at for its prediction? (perturbation experiment)
•a r e the most important features provided by explanations suﬃcient 
to make the model do its prediction? (suﬃciency experiment)
In particular, we consider three token perturbation strategies Zhang 
et al. (2020): (1) removal, (2) random modiﬁcation and (3) replace-
ment with the padding token. We perturb a percentage of the APIs 
per sequence considering the ascending/descending order of the scores 
assigned by explainers. Speciﬁcally, we perform two experiments: i) 
perturbation of the 1%, 5%and 10% of the most relevant APIs (pertur-
bation experiment); ii) perturbation of the 70%, 80%, 90%, 95%, 99%of 
the least relevant APIs (suﬃciency experiment).
In Table 6 (resp. Table 7) we report the mean changes in LSTM 
(resp. GRU) prediction probabilities when perturbing the 1% of the 
most important tokens (i.e., perturbation experiment) and the 99% of 
the least important tokens (i.e., suﬃciency experiment). What stands 
out is that the perturbation strategy (i.e. removal, random modiﬁcation, 
replacement) can have strong inﬂuence on the changes in prediction 
probabilities, but the comparison of explanation techniques is not inﬂu-
enced by such diﬀerences.

experiment, the attention is competitive with other techniques and, in 
the case Mal-API-2019 dataset, it even provides the best explanations 
because predictions do not change when perturbing useless features.
5.4.4. Stability
The explanations provided for the security domain should be reliable 
in order to be considered by expert analysts. In other words, the scores 
representing the system calls importance need to be stable over time: 
we expect homogeneous explanations for the same sequence or similar 
ones. Besides, from the XAI perspective, unstable explanations cannot 
express the real knowledge of the model.

intervals have been computed as well. Table8shows that LIME cannot 
provide stable explanations for any dataset and model, achieving very

5.5. Case study
We conduct a case study on the diﬀerent malware classes of the 
Alibaba dataset for showing how XAI analysis can support practitioners 
in the understanding of malicious behaviors. Fig.9shows the LRP scores 
assigned to API call sequences classiﬁed as Backdoor, Downloader, and 
Trojan, respectively.
•Fig. 9a (Backdoor) This attack aims at maintaining a connection 
with a remote host bypassing any network security mechanism 
and does not perform any peculiar malicious activity. The expla-
nation unveils this behavior due to the high relevance assigned to 
the InternetOpenA and InternetOpenUrlA calls, meaning that the 
malware is trying to connect via URL. On the contrary, the other 
API calls which represent the actual activity performed on the re-
mote host (e.g. CreateServiceand FindResource) are not considered 
important for the classiﬁcation;
•Fig. 9b (Downloader). A Downloader is a type of Trojan horse 
that downloads and installs malicious software without the con-
sent of the user. The explanation provided by LRP highlights the 
NtOpenDirectoryObjectcall  which tries to copy ﬁles from a part to 
another of the ﬁle system.11
•Fig. 9c (Trojan). A Trojan Horse malware downloads onto a com-
puter disguised as a legitimate program. Usually, the attacker hides 
malicious code within a legitimate software to gain access to the 
system of a user. In ﬁgure, we can notice that NtFreeVirtualMemory
and NtAllocateVirtualMemory calls have been assigned a high 
score, probably due to a buﬀer overﬂow tentative which is trying 
to work with virtual memory in order to inject commands. Further-
more, GetSystemTimeAsFileTimeis  a well-known part of malicious 
Trojan softwares.12
Speciﬁcally, this approach reduces the number of false positives by

by the service A (CreateServiceA) installed through the use of the back-
door (InternetOpenA), initiates the execution of a process that can be 
monitored through a hidden interface. The emphasis placed on the API 
call NtFreeVirtualMemory focuses the operator’s attention on previous 
processes that have allocated memory for instantiating a service. In ad-
dition, the malware analyst can more quickly unveil distinctive patterns

5.6. Discussion
Looking at the big picture, the data-driven nature of the CTI pro-
cess can be strongly improved with AI-based systems. Nevertheless, XAI 
methods and tools are essential to make the most recent AI advance-
ments applicable in real security scenarios, thus enhancing the need of 
jointly integrating the two elements into existing CTI platforms. From 
the end-user viewpoint, the idea is to replicate the same thinking pro-
cess a domain expert does when reaching a conclusion supported by 
the results of (a series of) statistical methods. From this perspective, the 
explanation should reveal those insights or at least guide the analyst to-
wards them. For instance, when discovering the system’s components 
aﬀected by a malware, the explanations could support the analyst in 
understanding what has happened, why it happened and which inter-
ventions to prioritize to restore the system.
The experiments conducted in the previous sections highlight that 
further research is needed in the XAI ﬁeld to achieve these goals. Firstly, 
the compatibility study shows that there is little accordance between 
XAI techniques, thus increasing the need to deﬁne ground truth expla-
nations for predictions.
Meanwhile, the explanation quality can be estimated indirectly by 
measuring how explanation-driven experiments impact on model per-
formance. From this perspective, SHAP established its superiority in 
both the perturbation and stability experiments but its limitation is 
clear when considering the eﬃciency: not only is the computation time 
higher w.r.t. other techniques, but also this gap increases exponen-
tially when considering more (training) data or longer sequences. On 
the other hand, LRP performs slightly worse on the perturbation ex-
periment but outperforms all other post-hoc methods when it comes to 
stability and time eﬃciency.
In general, LIME performs worse than SHAP and LRP and reveals its 
greatest limitation when considering the explanations’ stability: on two 
out of the three datasets we considered, the method is able to retrieve 
repeatedly less than the 10%of the top-ranked explanations.
The sequence-level representations obtained by scaling each API call 
embedding with the score assigned by the explainer before averaging all 
the embeddings, show that SHAP delivers the best structured represen-
tation, while LRP represents all the malwareinstances in a dense region.
Finally, the attention mechanism seems the worst technique for at 
least three reasons. First, even if it increases model complexity, it does 
not improve classiﬁcation performance. Second, it requires model re-
training and thus cannot be used with oﬀ-the-shelf models. Third, from 
the XAI perspective, even though the attention scores are consistent 
for not relevant features, they do not ﬁnd out the most important pat-
terns leading to the actual predictions. In complete fairness, we can 
also outline that our dataset might not be suited to understand whether

6. Conclusions & future work
In this paper, we have proposed an eXplainable Artiﬁcial Intelli-
gence framework for the behavioral malware detection problem. The 
main goal has been to evaluate the eﬀectiveness of four XAI methods 
applied to a sequence-based deep learning architecture, and their appli-
cability and suitability in the modern CS scenarios.
In particular, the reported experiments have shown, as highlighted 
in our discussions, the feasibility of diﬀerent XAI techniques for ex-
plaining the decision of the LSTM-based classiﬁer both in terms of 
explanations’ quality and running times necessary to obtain the related 
results. Furthermore, other experiments have proven the stability and 
ﬂexibility of the exploited tools. Note that we analyze localexplanation 
methods, i.e. methods in this work are able to explain predictions about 
single input samples. Global explanations are out of the scope of this 
work.
However, one of the main limitations of this analysis mainly con-
cerns the eﬀectiveness evaluation of XAI methodologies due to the 
lack of qualitative metrics to directly evaluate the eﬀectiveness of XAI 
methodologies. Another limitation may be related to the analyzed do-
main, that could aﬀect the main ﬁndings of the analysis.
Future work will be devoted to test additional XAI methods and the 
robustness of the proposed architecture with respect to adversarial at-
tacks and how such kind of perturbations can inﬂuence the quality of 
produced explanations. Furthermore, we wonder whether the explana-
tions can be exploited to improve the classiﬁcation performance even 
supporting the discovery of systematic errors of the predictive model. 
Finally, we will evaluate the explanations in real-world scenarios mea-
suring their eﬀectiveness and eﬃcacy w.r.t. how they improve or speed 
up the expert analyst’s work.
